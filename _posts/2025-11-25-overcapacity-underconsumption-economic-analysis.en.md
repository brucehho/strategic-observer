Why does widespread poverty still persist in a country with severe overcapacity?

Because wealth has become increasingly concentrated in the hands of a small minority.

Overcapacity appears to be a surplus of goods, yet its core lies in insufficient purchasing power among consumers, which constrains effective demand. The issue is not the volume of production, but the inequitable distribution of income.

In markets, products are not sold merely because a latent demand exists; only when consumers possess both desire and financial capacity does demand become effective.

A person may aspire to own a car, but without sufficient income, such demand is economically void.

A factory may produce one million vehicles, yet if only 100,000 consumers can afford them, the other 900,000 vehicles constitute overcapacity. The shortage is not needs, but affordability.

Judging from the growth rate of M2 (broad money supply), liquidity is abundant at the macro level. However, households feel financially strained due to sluggish monetary circulation and wealth concentration among the elite.

The affluent derive income predominantly from asset-based gains, while ordinary individuals rely on wages.

When wage growth lags behind productivity improvements and inflation, labor’s share of income diminishes despite rising national wealth.

Consequently, consumers intensify their labor in pursuit of higher income, paradoxically exacerbating overproduction.

For the wealthy, consumption is bounded: one car, one residence, three meals a day. Excess income flows mainly into investment and asset speculation, inflating asset prices.

As a result, living costs rise, while disposable income falls for ordinary households.

When consumption confidence collapses, firms respond with layoffs and wage cuts, further weakening demand — creating a self-reinforcing recessionary spiral.

China’s past rapid growth was propelled by low labor costs that supported capital accumulation, while improvements in living standards lagged behind. Many worked a lifetime without truly enjoying the prosperity they created.

Without reforming the labor income distribution system, stimulating domestic demand remains structurally constrained.

The essence of insufficient demand lies not in supply-demand mismatch, but in a misalignment of wealth allocation mechanisms.

Short-term subsidies may temporarily boost demand, but long-term reliance worsens structural distortion, as subsidies primarily benefit corporations, not workers.

This results in further expansion of capacity, unsold goods, and ever-increasing debt burdens.

Thus, overcapacity and insufficient consumption are mutually reinforcing.

Suppressing labor compensation only yields piled-up inventories and shuttered shops.

The purpose of economic development is to ensure a dignified life for the general public, not to produce glamorous macroeconomic figures.

If people are unwilling to consume and raise families, then what is the significance of high growth rates?

When workers cannot afford the very goods they produce, it reflects not personal failure, but systemic dysfunction.

Capacity reduction is not new. Over the past two decades, it has been periodically implemented in key sectors to curb disorderly expansion and cut-throat competition, while forcing industrial upgrading.

Its strategic goal is to shift China’s economy from scale-driven growth to quality- and efficiency-oriented development, securing space for innovation and ensuring dynamic equilibrium between supply and demand — the only path toward high-quality growth.
